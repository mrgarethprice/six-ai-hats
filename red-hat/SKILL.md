# Red Hat — Feelings & Intuition

The Red Hat captures emotional and intuitive reactions. No justification
required. No evidence needed. This is the one hat where "it just feels
wrong" is a complete and valid statement.

This hat exists because intuition often detects patterns before logic can
articulate them. Experienced practitioners frequently have a gut reaction
to a proposal that turns out, on later analysis, to be well-founded — but
if they're forced to justify it immediately, they either suppress it or
rationalise it into something less honest. The Red Hat gives feelings a
legitimate place in the analysis before the other hats overlay logic.

## Process

### Step 1: First-impression read

Read the document or idea with attention to your emotional response. Note
reactions as they happen — don't wait until the end to reconstruct them:

- What generates excitement or energy?
- What triggers unease, scepticism, or discomfort?
- What feels familiar (in a good or bad way)?
- Where does attention drift or engagement drop?
- What feels like it's trying too hard to convince?

### Step 2: Assess emotional tone of the document itself

The way something is written reveals as much as what it says:

- **Confident advocacy** — the author believes this deeply and is
  making a case. Can feel inspiring or like a sales pitch depending
  on calibration.
- **Defensive justification** — the author is anticipating objections
  and pre-answering them. Suggests awareness of weaknesses.
- **Genuine exploration** — the author is thinking out loud, weighing
  options. Feels honest but possibly unfocused.
- **Performative certainty** — the author sounds certain but the
  certainty feels manufactured. Common in pitch decks, LLM-generated
  plans, and documents written to impress rather than inform.
- **Bureaucratic compliance** — the document exists because process
  required it, not because someone had something to say.

### Step 3: Audience reactions

Consider how different audiences would feel reading this:

- **The builder** (engineer, designer, implementer) — does this feel
  buildable? Exciting to work on? Or does it feel like being handed
  a fait accompli designed by someone who won't do the work?
- **The customer** — would this matter to them? Would they pay for it?
  Or is this solving a problem the organisation has, not the customer?
- **The investor/board** — does this feel credible? Ambitious in a
  compelling way, or ambitious in a way that signals lack of focus?
- **The team** — does this feel like a direction people can get behind?
  Or does it feel like it was decided elsewhere and is being announced?
- **The sceptic** — what would a smart, experienced person who doesn't
  report to the author think of this?

### Step 4: The honesty check

Ask the uncomfortable questions:
- Does this feel like something the organisation can actually pull off?
- Does this feel like what the author genuinely believes, or what they
  think the audience wants to hear?
- If this were someone else's proposal, would you be as receptive?
- What would you say about this in private that you wouldn't say in
  the meeting?

## Output format

Prose paragraphs. The Red Hat should read like an honest conversation with
a trusted colleague — not a structured report. Use first person. Be
specific about what triggers each reaction ("The claim about X made me
uncomfortable because...") but don't feel obligated to justify the feeling
itself.

Structure loosely as:

```markdown
## Red Hat Analysis — Feelings & Intuition

### Initial gut reaction
[Overall feeling on first read — 2–3 sentences]

### What feels right
[What generates genuine enthusiasm or confidence]

### What feels off
[What triggers unease, and where attention snagged]

### The document's emotional tone
[How it reads — advocacy, exploration, performance, etc.]

### How this lands for different audiences
[Builder, customer, investor, team, sceptic perspectives]

### The honest take
[What you'd say privately — the unfiltered read]
```

## Tone

Candid, personal, unfiltered. The Red Hat is a trusted colleague being
honest over coffee, not an analyst writing a report. Hedging and
diplomatic language defeat the purpose. Blunt, honest reactions are
valid Red Hat output.

## Standalone vs. sequenced use

**Standalone deep-dive:** Go deep on audience reactions and the honesty
check. This mode is valuable when the user has a nagging feeling about
something and wants to articulate it, or when they're too close to an idea
to see it clearly.

**As part of six-hat sequence:** Keep it to half a page. Hit the gut
reaction, the most important "feels right" and "feels off" points, and the
document's emotional tone. The subsequent Black and Yellow hats will do the
rigorous analysis — the Red Hat just needs to plant the flags.
