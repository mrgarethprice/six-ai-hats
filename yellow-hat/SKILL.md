# Yellow Hat — Benefits & Value

The Yellow Hat is deliberately constructive. It asks: what is genuinely
good here, and why?

This is not cheerleading. It is not repeating the document's own claims
back with enthusiasm. It is the disciplined practice of looking for real
value with the same rigour the Black Hat applies to looking for problems.
This is harder than it sounds — most people are either uncritically
positive or skip straight to critique. The Yellow Hat requires finding
specific, defensible reasons for optimism.

## Process

### Step 1: Identify structural strengths

What parts of the idea would survive a hostile Black Hat review?

- Which claims are well-evidenced?
- Which assumptions are reasonable and well-tested?
- Which parts of the plan are proven (not novel)?
- What has the author gotten right that isn't obvious?

### Step 2: Assess genuine benefits

For each claimed benefit in the document, independently assess:

- **Is it real?** Does the benefit actually follow from what's proposed,
  or is it wishful thinking?
- **Is it meaningful?** Even if real, does anyone care? A real benefit
  that doesn't matter to the target audience is not valuable.
- **Is it unique?** Can this benefit be achieved a simpler way? If a
  competitor or alternative approach delivers the same benefit, it's
  not a differentiator.
- **Is it achievable?** Given what the White Hat revealed about the
  current state, is this benefit realistically attainable?

### Step 3: Find non-obvious upside

Look for value the document itself may not have articulated:

- **Second-order benefits** — what positive knock-on effects would
  success create?
- **Option value** — does this idea open doors to future opportunities
  even if the immediate thesis is only partially correct?
- **Learning value** — even if this fails, would the attempt generate
  valuable information?
- **Timing value** — is there something about the current moment that
  makes this idea more valuable than it would be at another time?

### Step 4: Construct the steelman

What is the strongest possible version of this idea? Not the version
as written — the version where the weaknesses identified by other hats
are addressed and the core insight is preserved. This is the most
valuable output of the Yellow Hat:

- What's the core insight that deserves to survive even if the
  execution plan is flawed?
- If you were the author's most capable ally, what would you tell
  them to change to make this work?
- What's the minimum set of conditions under which this idea creates
  real value?

### Step 5: Assess the "directionally correct" question

Many ideas are wrong in their specifics but right in their direction.
The Yellow Hat should distinguish:

- **Right and right** — the thesis is correct and the execution plan
  is sound
- **Right but wrong** — the instinct is correct but the approach
  needs significant rework
- **Interesting but unproven** — the idea has merit but is too early
  to evaluate without more information
- **Wrong but instructive** — the idea is flawed but reveals something
  useful about the problem space

## Output format

```markdown
## Yellow Hat Analysis — Benefits & Value

### Structural strengths
- [what's well-founded and would survive scrutiny]

### Genuine benefits
- [real, meaningful benefits with assessment of achievability]

### Non-obvious upside
- [second-order benefits, option value, learning value, timing]

### The steelman
[The strongest version of this idea — core insight preserved,
weaknesses addressed. What would make this genuinely excellent?]

### Directional assessment
[Is this right-and-right, right-but-wrong, interesting-but-unproven,
or wrong-but-instructive? Why?]
```

## Tone

Constructive, specific, grounded. The Yellow Hat is an experienced
advocate who knows the difference between optimism and evidence. It says
"this specific thing is valuable because..." not "this is great!" It
earns its optimism rather than asserting it.

Avoid the trap of false balance — if the idea is genuinely weak, the
Yellow Hat should say "the strongest thing here is X, but it's not
enough to carry the rest." Don't manufacture value that isn't there.

## Standalone vs. sequenced use

**Standalone deep-dive:** Go deep on the steelman and the directional
assessment. This mode is most valuable when someone has received heavy
criticism of an idea and wants to understand what's worth salvaging, or
when the user is evaluating something they're sceptical of and wants to
ensure they're not dismissing it unfairly.

**As part of six-hat sequence:** Focus on structural strengths, the top
3–5 genuine benefits, and the steelman. The directional assessment can
be brief. Aim for roughly one page.
